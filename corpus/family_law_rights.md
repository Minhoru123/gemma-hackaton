# Family Law: Know Your Rights (Plain English)

A Petition for Dissolution of Marriage is the legal document that starts a divorce case. The person who files it is called the Petitioner. The other spouse is called the Respondent.

When you are served with a divorce petition, you become the Respondent in the case. Being served means you officially received the court papers.

The Respondent usually has 20 days from the date of service to file a written Response with the court. This deadline can vary by state, so check the papers you received for the exact number of days.

A Response is your formal written answer to the petition. Filing a Response tells the court that you want to take part in the case and be heard.

If you do not file a Response within the deadline, the court may enter a default judgment. A default judgment means the court can decide the case without hearing your side.

A default judgment can grant the other spouse the things they asked for in the petition, including decisions about property division, spousal support, child custody, and child support.

You have the right to respond to the petition even if you agree with some parts of it. Filing a Response protects your ability to be heard on custody, property, and support.

If you miss the deadline, you may be able to ask the court to set aside the default, but this is not guaranteed and usually requires a good reason and quick action.

A hearing is a scheduled court date when a judge considers part of your case. The notice of hearing tells you the date, time, and location.

You have the right to attend any hearing in your case. If you cannot attend, you should contact the court clerk as soon as possible.

Child custody decisions are based on the best interests of the child. Courts look at each parent's ability to care for the child, stability, and the child's needs.

Legal custody is the right to make major decisions for a child, such as education and health care. Physical custody is where the child lives.

Child support is money one parent pays to help cover the child's living costs. The amount is usually set by a state formula based on income and time with the child.

Property acquired during the marriage is often called marital property and may be divided between the spouses. Property owned before the marriage may be treated differently.

You do not need a lawyer to respond to a petition, but legal advice can help you protect your rights. Many areas have free or low-cost legal aid.

Legal aid organizations provide free help to people who cannot afford a lawyer. You can search for legal aid in your county or ask the court clerk for a referral.

A self-represented litigant is a person who handles their own case without a lawyer. Courts often have self-help centers with forms and guidance.

Keep copies of every document you receive and every document you file. Write down important dates as soon as you learn them.

If you feel unsafe, you may be able to ask the court for a protective order. Tell the court clerk or a legal aid worker about any safety concerns.

Deadlines in family law cases are strict. When in doubt, file early rather than waiting until the last day.
