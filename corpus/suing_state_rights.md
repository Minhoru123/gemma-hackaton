# Suing the State or a Government Agency: Know Your Rights (Plain English)

Suing a government agency is different from suing a private person or company. Special rules and shorter deadlines usually apply.

Many states require you to file a notice of claim before you can sue a government agency. A notice of claim is a formal written warning that tells the government you intend to sue.

The deadline to file a notice of claim is often very short, sometimes as little as 30, 60, or 90 days after the event. Missing this deadline can permanently end your right to sue.

The notice of claim usually must describe what happened, when and where it happened, and the harm or injury you suffered.

Sovereign immunity is a legal rule that can protect governments from some lawsuits. Laws called tort claims acts create exceptions that let people sue the government in certain situations.

Because of sovereign immunity, there are limits on when and how you can sue the state. These limits are strict and are enforced by the courts.

You generally file your notice of claim with the specific agency involved, or with a designated state office. The correct place to file is set by law.

Keep proof of when and how you filed your notice of claim, such as a certified mail receipt or a date-stamped copy.

After filing a notice of claim, there is often a waiting period before you can file a lawsuit. The government may investigate or respond during this time.

Damages you can recover against a government agency may be capped by law. A cap is a legal limit on the amount of money you can receive.

Deadlines for suing the government, called statutes of limitations, are often shorter than for suing a private party. Confirm the exact deadline that applies to your case.

You have the right to represent yourself when suing a government agency, but these cases are complex and legal help is strongly recommended.

If you cannot afford a lawyer, legal aid organizations and law school clinics may be able to help with claims against the government.

Read every letter or notice from the government agency carefully, because it may contain deadlines that affect your rights.

When in doubt about a government claim deadline, act quickly and seek legal advice, because these deadlines are rarely extended.
